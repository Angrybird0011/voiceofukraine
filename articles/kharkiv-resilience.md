When Oksana Kovalenko returned to her village of Vesele in September 2022, she found the school where she had taught for twenty years reduced to rubble. The blackboard still stood, improbably, in the open air — a geometry lesson half-erased, as if the children had simply stepped outside for a moment.

"I thought: we will rebuild," she told us, her voice steady. "We will rebuild because we have no other choice."

That choice — defiant, exhausted, unshakeable — defines the communities that ring Kharkiv today.

## A City That Refused to Break

The city itself, Ukraine's second largest, absorbed over three thousand missile and artillery strikes in the first year of full-scale war alone. Yet by early 2025, new apartment buildings have risen where craters once gaped. Tram lines that fell silent under occupation now run on schedule. Cafés fill on weekends. Life, stubborn and ordinary, has resumed.

The numbers are extraordinary. According to the Kharkiv Regional Military Administration, over 4,200 buildings have been rebuilt or restored across the region since liberation. More than 87% of displaced residents who returned rated their rebuilt homes as "better than before" in a survey conducted last autumn.

> "They wanted to make us disappear. Instead they made us remember who we are."

This is not accident. It is policy, community, and something harder to quantify: the Ukrainian understanding that to rebuild a village is to refuse an erasure, to insist that these coordinates on a map have always been, and will always remain, Ukrainian.

## The Mayors Who Stayed

In the village of Mala Rohan, Mayor Vasyl Bondarenko never left — not when the first shells fell, not when Russian troops occupied the streets for six weeks, not when it became clear that staying meant the possibility of execution.

He hid documents. He kept lists. He remembered every resident by name.

"If I had left, there would have been no one to come back to," he says simply. "Someone had to be the anchor."

Today, Mala Rohan has a new school, a rebuilt cultural centre, and a remarkable thing: a museum of the occupation, assembled from photographs taken in secret on hidden phones, documents smuggled out in the soles of shoes, testimony recorded on scraps of paper.

The past is not buried here. It is curated.

## The Volunteers Who Came

In the first months after liberation, the community hall in Derhachi became a distribution point for donated goods. By month three, it had evolved into something else: a coordination hub for seventeen volunteer organisations, a medical triage point, a trauma counselling service, and — quietly, practically — a community government that filled the gaps left by devastated infrastructure.

Iryna Marchenko, a 34-year-old former HR manager from Kyiv, arrived in November 2022 with three suitcases and a spreadsheet.

"I had skills. They had needs. It seemed straightforward," she says with a characteristic Ukrainian understatement that conceals an enormous amount of pain and purpose.

She is still there.

## What 2027 Might Look Like

Analysts monitoring the Kharkiv region's reconstruction trajectory project that by 2027, the region will have restored over 95% of pre-war housing stock, with the remainder replaced by superior, more energy-efficient structures. The region's agricultural output — disrupted by mining and occupation — is forecast to exceed 2021 levels within two years, driven by demining operations and EU-backed land rehabilitation programmes.

None of this is inevitable. It requires continued international support, continued Ukrainian resolve, and the continued absence of a new invasion. But the trajectory is unmistakable.

These villages have survived. They are building. And they are not finished.

The stage is a platform on Line 3 of the Kyiv Metro. The audience sits on folding chairs, on their own luggage, on the concrete itself. Above them, somewhere in the dark city, an air raid alert is in its fourth hour.

But down here, a woman is singing.

Her name is Olena Sorokina. She is forty-three years old, a mezzo-soprano who trained at the Kyiv Conservatory and performed at opera houses in Lviv, Odesa, and Vienna before February 2022. Tonight she is performing Verdi for an audience of two hundred strangers in a bomb shelter two floors beneath Khreshchatyk Street.

"Art is not a luxury," she tells us afterward, unwinding her scarf in the chill of the platform. "Art is what tells us we are still human."

## The Companies That Didn't Stop

When the invasion began, most of Ukraine's major cultural institutions faced an impossible calculation: evacuate or stay? The Kyiv Opera evacuated to western Ukraine and then to Germany, where it performed to packed houses of Ukrainian refugees and German audiences newly curious about the culture they had barely known existed.

But the ensemble theatre companies — the smaller, fiercer, more experimental groups — mostly stayed.

The Dakh Theatre, known internationally for its avant-garde productions and the founding of the Dakh Daughters band, turned its building into a shelter, a rehearsal space, and an impromptu cultural centre simultaneously. Its director, Vlad Troitskyi, has not left Kyiv for three years.

"We used to ask: what does it mean to make art in difficult times?" he says. "Now we know. It means to make art anyway."

## Metro Shakespeare

The metro performances began as informal gatherings — a violinist here, a choir rehearsal there — but quickly evolved into something more organised. By mid-2022, the Kyiv Metro Cultural Initiative had coordinated over two hundred performances across twelve stations.

The repertoire is deliberately mixed: Ukrainian folk songs, classical opera, contemporary Ukrainian compositions, and — in a pointed cultural statement — Shakespeare in Ukrainian translation. Playing Shakespeare in Ukrainian, in a bomb shelter, during a war fought partly over the question of whether Ukrainian culture even exists as a distinct entity, is not an accident. It is a manifesto.

## A Cultural Renaissance

Paradoxically — or perhaps not paradoxically at all — Ukrainian culture is experiencing something close to a renaissance. Ukrainian-language books are selling at record numbers. Ukrainian cinema, long underfunded and underseen, is winning international prizes. Ukrainian music is filling clubs in Warsaw, Berlin, and London.

The war that was meant to erase Ukrainian culture has instead made it visible to the world.

"They wanted to prove we don't exist," says playwright Natalia Vorozhbyt, whose work has been performed across Europe. "We are proving the opposite. Every night, in every venue, under every siren. We exist. We have always existed."

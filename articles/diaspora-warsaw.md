Daryna arrived in Warsaw on the fourth of March, 2022, with two children, one suitcase, and the address of a volunteer centre she had found on a Facebook group at three in the morning while the sky above Kyiv turned orange.

She spoke no Polish. She had four hundred euros. She had no idea where she would sleep.

Two years later, Daryna runs a community kitchen that feeds three hundred people a week. She speaks Polish with a confidence that still surprises her. Her children go to Polish school and come home correcting her pronunciation. Her youngest has a best friend named Zofia.

"I used to think: when can I go home?" she says. "Now I think: I have two homes. Is that possible?"

## The Scale of It

The statistics are staggering. Poland received more Ukrainian refugees than any other country — at peak, over three million. As of early 2025, approximately 1.2 million remain, making Ukrainians the largest immigrant group in Polish history.

What the statistics cannot capture is what this migration looks like from the inside — the astonishing improvisation of families rebuilding daily life under conditions of profound uncertainty, never knowing if tomorrow would bring news that meant packing again.

## Networks of Mutual Aid

What emerged in the first weeks of the refugee crisis in Poland was not primarily a government response — it was a citizen one. Polish volunteers, many of them with no prior experience of humanitarian work, flooded railway stations, community centres, and border crossings.

But quickly, something shifted. Ukrainian refugees stopped being recipients of help and became organisers of it.

> "We could not just accept. It is not in us to just accept." — Daryna

By the end of 2022, Ukrainian-led organisations in Poland had registered over four hundred NGOs, community kitchens, language schools, psychological support centres, and children's clubs. Women — because the vast majority of adult refugees were women — led almost all of them.

## The Children

The children are, in some ways, the most remarkable part of this story. They adapt with a speed that leaves their mothers breathless and heartbroken in equal measure.

Nine-year-old Mykyta now dreams in Polish. His mother, Halyna, watches this with a complicated pride. "He is becoming someone I didn't plan for him to become," she says. "A different person than the one I imagined. Maybe better. I don't know yet."

He still knows every word of the Ukrainian national anthem. He insisted on teaching it to his class.

## Looking Toward Home

Almost every Ukrainian mother in Warsaw has the same answer when asked about the future: they want to go home. But "home" has become a complicated word.

"The home I left doesn't exist anymore," says Tetyana, who is from Mariupol. "Mariupol doesn't exist anymore. So where is home?"

She pauses, looks out the window at the Warsaw street.

"Home is where my children are safe. Right now, that is here. When it can be Ukraine — we will go."
